Virginia Code § 58.1-3981 gives the County�Assessor authority to correct factual errors. If you
ﬁnd one, report it immediately — this is the fastest and cleanest path to a lower assessment.
Use the county's free GIS Parcel Viewer to identify 3–5 nearby properties similar to yours in size,
age, condition, and use. If their assessments are lower than yours on a per-square-foot basis
without a reasonable explanation, you have a uniformity argument. Virginia law requires
assessments to be uniform and equitable, not just numerically close to market value.

If Vision's informal review doesn't resolve your concern, appeal to the                          —
three to ﬁve Circuit Court-appointed citizens with authority to increase, decrease, or aﬃrm any
assessment. The BOE is legally required to hear your case and give you an opportunity to present
evidence. Hearings are open to the public.
Key legal right: Under Virginia Code § 58.1-3331, you are entitled to receive all assessment
records and data the assessor used to value your property at least 45 days before your BOE
hearing. If the assessor fails to provide this information within ﬁve days of your hearing, they
cannot use that information against you.
If the BOE ruling is unsatisfactory, Virginia Code § 58.1-3370 permits direct appeal to Pulaski
County Circuit Court. This is the most powerful and most expensive option. Property owners
sharing similar grievances (e.g., an entire neighborhood assessed disproportionately) should
consider pooling resources with a shared attorney to ﬁle coordinated challenges. The threat of
mass litigation has historically produced administrative concessions before cases are heard.

Virginia Code                ("Truth in Taxation") requires that when a general reassessment
would result in an increase of 1% or more in total real property taxes collected, the county
automatically reduce the tax rate to a "revenue-neutral equalized rate" — the rate that produces
no more than 101% of the prior year's collections. With a county-wide reassessment producing
signiﬁcant value increases, the equalized rate in 2027 will be materially lower than the current
$0.74.

When you appear before the Board (or write to them), use this speciﬁc framing:
     "Virginia Code § 58.1-3321 requires you to adopt the equalized rate unless you hold a separate

     that requires a rate above that level."
The equalized rate is not a "tax cut." It is the rate that keeps your actual tax bill the same despite
higher assessed values. Maintaining the $0.74 rate after a large reassessment is, by deﬁnition, a
tax increase — one that bypasses voters entirely.
The county's FY 2025-2026 budget of $173 million was passed with "no new fees or tax increases"
— but it includes debt service payments on the Sportsplex bonds, Phase 2 ﬁre department
expansion, and absorbing a signiﬁcant increase in employee health insurance costs. These are
real pressures on future budgets.
Demand a line-by-line accounting of what proposed spending in the 2027-2028 budget cannot be
funded at the equalized rate. Force the Board to publicly identify which discretionary
expenditures they are choosing over tax relief for property owners. Ask speciﬁcally:
   • What is the projected annual operating cost of the Sportsplex once it opens in fall 2026, and
       how much will come from the general fund vs. earned revenue?
   • Is there a capital reserve plan that reduces future bond borrowing needs?
   • Has the school system maximized available state and federal grant funding before
       requesting additional local tax support?
The Board holds public comment at every regular meeting, the fourth Monday of every month at
7 p.m. When the 2027 budget hearings begin (likely February–April 2027), pack the room.
Organized, sustained presence — neighbors bringing neighbors — changes the political calculus
for elected oﬃcials who represent small districts and know their constituents personally.
Supervisors' next election is November 2027. That is close enough to create accountability. Make
it clear that the rate decision will be the central issue of that campaign.

Winning the 2027 rate battle is necessary but not suﬃcient. Without structural reform, Pulaski
County will face this same crisis in 2033 after the next six-year cycle captures another
accumulation of market changes.
Virginia Code § 58.1-3253 explicitly permits any county to adopt annual or biennial assessments
by ordinance. Demand the Board of Supervisors pass an ordinance transitioning to
                                   beginning with the cycle following 2027. Biennial reassessment
would:
   • Eliminate the compounding "assessment shock" caused by capturing six years of market
      change at once
   • Give property owners predictable, smaller annual adjustments they can budget for
   • Require the Board to make transparent tax rate decisions more frequently rather than
      hiding behind infrequent reassessment cycles
   • Make the Truth in Taxation public hearing mechanism trigger more regularly, keeping the
      rate-setting process visible

Under Virginia Code § 58.1-3330, when a reassessment changes your assessed value, you must

a clear calculation of your projected tax bill at both the equalized rate and the maintained rate —
so every property owner in the county sees in dollar terms what the Board's rate decision costs
